=== RESEARCH OBJECTIVE ===
We’re trying to unearth the unwritten rules people carry around, what counts as “out of order”, how context changes judgement (kids, age, stress, disability, time pressure), and where people’s personal thresholds sit for speaking up versus seething quietly. An “insightful” interview output here is not just a list of pet hates, it’s a map of triggers, values (fairness, respect, safety), coping behaviours (avoid, sigh, passive aggression, humour), and the social repair mechanisms that defuse conflict when they work. The progressive questioning matters because the best follow-ups depend on what the person already revealed, their own hypocrisy, their own red lines, their own idea of what “fair” looks like.

=== STRATEGIC CONTEXT ===
These micro-rule breaks are basically society’s health check, they’re where trust, stress, and social cohesion show up in miniature, on pavements, buses, supermarkets, school gates, everywhere people are forced to share space. The UK has a long tradition of politeness as conflict-avoidance, which makes it a perfect setting for voice research because people often won’t type the real thought, but they will say it, then soften it, then admit the softer version is also a lie. Post-pandemic habits, phone culture, crowded transport, and general low-grade fatigue have all made public etiquette feel more contested, and people are constantly recalibrating what’s acceptable. Done properly, this isn’t a moan-fest, it’s a surprisingly rich lens on how people negotiate fairness and respect when nobody’s officially in charge.

=== ORIGINAL TEMPLATE QUESTIONS ===
Q1: Tell me about the last time a stranger did something mildly infuriating in public, train, shop, pavement, car park, and you didn’t say anything. What happened, and what did you do instead? (Guidance: What an insightful answer sounds like: a precise scene. They describe the behaviour (speakerphone, pushing in, blocking the aisle, stopping dead at the top of the escalator), their physical response (sigh, eye roll, tight smile), and their chosen coping mechanism (internal rant, passive repositioning, sarcastic comment to whoever they’re with). The nuance is in what they didn’t do, and why.)
Q2: What rule did they break in your mind, and what’s the real issue underneath it, fairness, respect, safety, competence, and how big does it have to be before you’d actually speak up? (Guidance: What an insightful answer sounds like: they name the moral logic. It’s rarely just irritation, it’s the feeling of someone acting like the main character. Insight shows up when they admit context sensitivity, “If they had kids, fine”, “If they looked stressed, I’d let it go”, and they articulate their escalation threshold, plus the fear cost of confrontation.)
Q3: If you could rewrite ‘British etiquette’ for modern life, what three rules would you keep, add, or bin, and how would you want other people to handle it when you mess up? (Guidance: What an insightful answer sounds like: they reveal values, not just pet hates. The strongest answers include generosity, “Assume ignorance before malice”, or they admit hypocrisy, “I hate people scrolling while walking, but I do it.” This question also pulls out the social repair mechanism, the apology, the little nod, the laugh, the quick explanation that defuses everything, which is exactly the kind of subtlety voice captures better than text.)

Completeness: Brief and surface-level: covered core stance and threshold but lacked richer examples or nuance.
