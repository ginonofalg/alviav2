# Open Britain — Information Landscape Research: Project Write-Up

## Background

Open Britain is a UK not-for-profit movement, hundreds of thousands of members strong, whose mission is to make democracy work for everyone. The organisation was incorporated in 2015 as the legal entity behind Britain Stronger in Europe — the official Remain campaign in the 2016 EU referendum. After the Leave vote, it rebranded as Open Britain in August 2016 and has since evolved from a single-issue post-referendum campaign into a broader pro-democracy movement organised around three pillars: electoral reform, eliminating dark money from politics, and combating disinformation.

Open Britain's most visible recent achievement is the establishment of the All-Party Parliamentary Group for Fair Elections, registered in October 2024 and publicly launched the following month with 150 MPs and peers from across the political spectrum — Labour, Conservative, Liberal Democrat, Green, and SNP. The APPG's inaugural report, *Free But Not Fair: British Elections and How to Restore Trust in Politics*, calls for a national commission on electoral reform and argues that trust in politics has reached rock bottom. Open Britain funds Fair Vote UK to act as the APPG's secretariat and shares leadership through its CEO, Mark Kieran, who also serves as Acting Director of Fair Vote UK.

The organisation sits within a wider ecosystem of UK democratic reform groups — including Make Votes Matter, Unlock Democracy, the Electoral Reform Society, and the Alliance Now coalition — but it occupies a distinctive position as the bridge between parliamentary engagement (through the APPG) and grassroots campaigning. Its journey from Remain campaign to structural reform movement reflects a broader pattern in UK civil society: the post-Brexit pivot from contesting the referendum result to addressing the systemic democratic failures that, in the view of these organisations, made it possible.

On disinformation specifically, Open Britain's work has historically focused on policy advocacy rather than public-facing tools. Its predecessor APPG on Electoral Campaigning Transparency produced *Defending Our Democracy in the Digital Age* in 2020, a report with 20 recommendations drawn from evidence by 70+ organisations (including Facebook, the ICO, and the Electoral Commission), targeting micro-targeting of voters, campaign spending opacity, and the Electoral Commission's enforcement powers. More recently, Open Britain highlighted the role of social media platforms in spreading the conspiracy theories that fuelled the August 2024 wave of racist violence across England — an event that underlined the real-world consequences of unchecked online disinformation.

Open Britain is interested in tools that operates at the individual citizen level; something that helps ordinary people navigate the information environment they live in, rather than advocating for top-down regulatory change. That gap is the starting point for this research.

## Why This Research Exists

Initial conversations with Open Britain demonstrate a need to explore tool to help people *think through issues where disinformation or media bias may have skewed public understanding — without pushing any particular political view*. The non-partisan constraint is critical. Open Britain's electoral reform and anti-dark-money work is openly campaigning; it has a position and advocates for it. But a tool that helps citizens evaluate political information cannot do the same thing. The moment it steers people toward a conclusion, it becomes another source of bias.

This creates a genuinely hard design problem. Fact-checkers already exist (Full Fact, BBC Verify, INFACT), but they operate after the fact, debunking individual claims. What Open Britain is describing is something upstream of fact-checking; a tool that helps people recognise the patterns in how they consume and evaluate political information before they encounter specific claims. That requires understanding the real information ecosystem citizens live in: where they get political information, how they decide what's trustworthy, what happens when they discover they were wrong, and what they actually want from a tool that doesn't tell them what to think.

This research exists to map that ecosystem from the citizen's perspective. The 2024 general election - which Open Britain describes as the least proportional in modern UK history - and the 2024 riots demonstrated that the consequences of information failure are not abstract. But the solution cannot be another campaign or report directed at Westminster. If Open Britain is going to build something that works at the individual level, they need to understand how individuals actually relate to political information.

The research is exploratory by design. Its aim is to surface the raw material; the habits, blind spots, self-awareness, and unmet needs,  from which tools or processes could be designed. 

## Project Configuration

**Project name:** How People Navigate Political Information

**Project objective:** Understand how UK citizens encounter, evaluate, and form views on politically charged issues; where their information comes from, how they assess what's trustworthy, and how media framing or disinformation may have shaped their understanding without them realising.

**Audience context:** UK adults across the political spectrum who follow news and current affairs to varying degrees; from daily news consumers to those who mostly pick things up through social media or conversation.

**Strategic context:** This research supports Open Britain's mission to help people think through issues where disinformation or media bias may have distorted public understanding. The goal is to surface the patterns in how people process political information, where trust breaks down, and what would actually help citizens feel more confident in their own judgement. Insights will inform the design of a public-facing tool for democratic education.

**Context type:** CX — because this is fundamentally about the citizen's experience of the political information landscape, not about a product, content strategy, or marketing campaign.

**Tone:** Conversational; political information habits are personal and sometimes uncomfortable to discuss honestly. A formal tone would push people toward performing media literacy rather than revealing their actual behaviour. Conversational warmth creates the safety needed for people to admit things like "I don't really check" or "I believed it because it felt right."

**Cross-interview context:** Enabled. This is essential for a research project about information patterns. The most interesting findings will emerge from comparing how different people in different parts of the political spectrum navigate the same landscape. When Alvia can recognise emerging themes; "several people have mentioned this same blind spot" or "you're the first person who described checking sources that way", she can probe more precisely and surface whether a given pattern is widespread or idiosyncratic.

**PII summary redaction:** Enabled. Note that individual transcripts retain these details. People discussing political views, media consumption, and moments where they were wrong or misled are sharing sensitive personal information. They may name journalists, politicians, family members, or friends who influenced their views. Redaction protects respondents and ensures the research can be shared with Open Britain's wider team without privacy concerns.

## Template Design

**Template name:** Information Landscape Deep Dive

**Template objective:** Understand how UK citizens encounter, evaluate, and form views on politically charged issues; where their information comes from, how they assess what's trustworthy, and how media framing or disinformation may have shaped their understanding without them realising. Surface the real information ecosystem each person lives in. Get past what they think they should say about media, and get to how they actually encounter and evaluate political claims, and the moments where they've realised something they believed was wrong or misleading.

The template contains five questions (four open and one scale) designed to move from personal belief archaeology (where did your views come from?) through moments of correction (when were you wrong?) and real behaviour (what do you actually do?) to self-assessment (how confident are you?) and finally the design question (what would help?). The arc is deliberately structured to make the final question — what a useful tool would look like — grounded in the respondent's own concrete experiences rather than abstract speculation.

---

### Question 1: Belief Origins

**Question:** "Think about a political issue you feel quite strongly about. Where did your view on that actually come from — can you trace it back?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 80 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** Get them to pick ONE specific issue and genuinely try to trace their view back to its origins. Most people will start with "I just think..."; push past that. Was it a news story? A conversation? Something on social media? A personal experience? The gold is in the gap between what they believe and how little they may know about where that belief originated. Don't challenge the view itself — stay curious about the journey.

**Rationale:** This question maps the supply chain of belief formation. Most people hold political views they've never interrogated the origins of — views that feel like their own but may have been shaped by a headline, a social media algorithm, a family conversation, or a cultural assumption they absorbed without noticing. For Open Britain's purposes, this is foundational data: if people can't trace where their views came from, they're unlikely to recognise when those views have been shaped by disinformation or bias. The question also establishes whether people are aware of the gap between "what I believe" and "why I believe it" — and how comfortable they are sitting with that gap. A tool designed to help people think more clearly about political issues has to start from an honest picture of how little most people know about their own belief formation processes.

---

### Question 2: Moments of Correction

**Question:** "Has there been a time when you discovered that something you'd believed about a political issue turned out to be wrong or misleading? What happened?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 70 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This is the hardest question. Nobody enjoys admitting they were wrong. If they say "not really", try: "What about something where you later found out the full picture was more complicated than you'd first heard?" Accept partial examples. Listen for HOW they discovered it — did someone tell them, did they stumble on it, did the story just evolve? The mechanism of correction matters as much as the example. Stay completely non-judgmental.

**Rationale:** This is the question most directly relevant to disinformation's real-world impact. It asks people to recall a moment where the information ecosystem failed them — where something they believed turned out to be wrong, misleading, or incomplete. The example itself matters, but the *mechanism of correction* matters more. Did they discover the error through their own research? Through a conversation with someone who disagreed? Through a subsequent news story? Through a fact-checker? Or did the correction just happen passively as the story evolved? Each mechanism implies a different intervention point for a tool. If most correction happens through social contact, a tool that works in isolation may miss the mark. If most people can't think of a single example, that suggests either remarkable media literacy or — more likely — a lack of awareness that they've been misled, which is itself a critical finding. The non-judgmental framing is essential: the question asks "what happened" not "how could you have been so wrong."

---

### Question 3: Real Verification Behaviour

**Question:** "When you see a claim about politics that you're not sure about, what do you actually do? Walk me through it honestly."

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 60 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** People will want to say "I check multiple sources"; push for what they ACTUALLY do, not what they think the right answer is. Do they scroll past? Google it? Ask someone? Accept it if it feels right? The honest answer is often "nothing" or "it depends" and that's fine — explore what "depends" means. Look for the difference between issues they care about (where they might check) and issues they don't (where they just absorb).

**Rationale:** This is the behaviour question, and the word "honestly" is doing real work. There is an enormous gap between what people say they do with uncertain political claims and what they actually do — a gap well-documented in media literacy research. Most people know they *should* check multiple sources, cross-reference, look for primary evidence. Very few actually do so consistently. For tool design, this gap is the most actionable data point in the entire template. If people's real behaviour when encountering uncertain claims is to scroll past, absorb it passively, or accept it based on whether it aligns with existing beliefs, then a tool that requires active engagement (go to this website, enter the claim, read the analysis) will fail. The tool has to meet people where their actual behaviour is, not where they wish it were. The distinction between issues people care about and issues they don't is equally important — it reveals the threshold of motivation required to trigger any verification behaviour at all.

---

### Question 4: Confidence Calibration

**Question:** "On a scale of 1 to 5, how confident are you that you can tell the difference between reliable and unreliable political information?"

**Type:** Scale (1-5) | **Time hint:** 50 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** The number opens the door; the conversation after it is what matters. High confidence (4-5): ask what gives them that confidence, then gently probe with "Is there any area where you feel less sure?" Low confidence (1-2): ask what specifically makes it hard — is it volume, contradictions, distrust of all sources? Mid-range (3): often the most interesting; ask what tips something from "I'm not sure" to "I believe this". Connect back to the examples they gave earlier.

**Rationale:** The scale question creates a quantitative anchor that can be cross-referenced against the qualitative data from the other four questions. A respondent who rates themselves a 5 but struggled to identify any moment of correction (Q2) and admits to rarely checking claims (Q3) presents a very different profile from a respondent who rates themselves a 2 but demonstrated sophisticated evaluation habits. This calibration gap — the difference between perceived and actual media literacy — is one of the most important variables for tool design. Over-confident users may resist a tool they feel they don't need. Under-confident users may be the most receptive audience but hardest to reach. The follow-up conversation also surfaces what people *mean* by reliable and unreliable — whether they're thinking about factual accuracy, political bias, tone, or the credibility of the source. These distinctions matter because a tool could address any of them, and people's implicit definitions shape what they'd find useful.

---

### Question 5: The Design Question

**Question:** "If something existed that could help you think through a political issue more clearly — without telling you what to think — what would that actually look like for you?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 70 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This is the design question — it directly informs what Open Britain might build. Resist letting them describe a fact-checker (that already exists). Push toward: what format? When would they use it? What would make them trust it? Would they want to be challenged or just given context? Listen for emotional needs as much as functional ones — do they want to feel smarter, more confident, less manipulated? If they say "I don't know", try: "Think about that moment from earlier when you weren't sure about something — what would have helped right then?"

**Rationale:** This question translates the preceding four questions' findings into design input. By this point in the conversation, the respondent has explored their own belief formation (Q1), recalled a moment of being wrong (Q2), confronted their actual verification behaviour (Q3), and assessed their confidence (Q4). The design question is deliberately placed last because the preceding conversation makes the respondent a more informed and honest interlocutor — they're no longer speculating in the abstract about media literacy tools; they're reflecting on their own concrete experiences and needs. The constraint "without telling you what to think" is load-bearing: it mirrors Open Britain's non-partisan commitment and forces respondents to imagine something genuinely different from a fact-checker or a politically aligned news source. The interviewer guidance to push past "a fact-checker" is critical because that's the default answer people reach for, and it already exists. The value is in whatever comes after that — the unmet needs that existing tools don't address.

## Collection Configuration

**Collection name:** Open Britain — Pilot Conversations

**Description:** Initial pilot with diverse UK citizens to understand information consumption patterns, susceptibility to media bias, and appetite for a non-partisan democratic education tool.

**Target responses:** 12 — enough to surface recurring patterns across different information consumption profiles while keeping the pilot lean. The diversity that matters here is not demographic but informational: heavy news consumers vs. social-media-only; people who trust mainstream media vs. those who don't; people across the political spectrum. Twelve conversations, with cross-interview context enabled, should reveal whether there are distinct "information personas" worth exploring further.

**Voice provider:** OpenAI

**Additional questions:** Up to 2 — allows Barbara to pursue threads that emerge naturally from each conversation. Given the subject matter, the most productive additional questions are likely to probe specific examples that the respondent mentioned in passing but didn't fully explore — a particular news story they were misled by, a specific source they trust or distrust, or a moment where they changed someone else's mind (or failed to). The flexibility also lets Barbara explore whether respondents have encountered deliberate disinformation campaigns or whether their information failures are more about passive absorption of framing and bias.

**End-of-interview summary:** Enabled — generates a structured summary of themes, engagement quality, and objective satisfaction after each conversation. Particularly valuable here because the interviews will range widely across different political topics and information sources; the summaries provide a consistent analytical layer across diverse content.

**VAD eagerness:** Auto — these are thoughtful, sometimes uncomfortable topics where people need space to think. Questions about belief origins, being wrong, and real behaviour (as opposed to aspirational behaviour) require pauses for honest reflection. High eagerness would risk cutting people off during exactly the moments where they're working through something difficult and the most valuable data is about to emerge. Standard sensitivity gives respondents the room to sit with uncomfortable realisations without the AI jumping in.

## Overall Design Rationale

The five questions follow a deliberate arc that serves both research rigour and conversational psychology:

1. **Archaeology before aspiration** — the template starts by asking people to excavate where their beliefs actually came from (Q1) and when they've been wrong (Q2) before asking what they do about it (Q3) or what they want (Q5). This ordering is intentional. If the design question came first, respondents would speculate abstractly. By the time they reach Q5, they've spent fifteen minutes confronting their own information habits and have specific, personal material to draw on. The conversation educates the respondent about their own patterns, which makes their design input dramatically more grounded.

2. **Non-partisan by design** — no question asks about specific political parties, policies, or positions. The focus is entirely on information *processes* — how people encounter, evaluate, form, and occasionally correct their political views. This is not an accident; it's the central design constraint. Open Britain's tool cannot push a political view, so the research that informs it cannot either. A respondent who voted Reform and a respondent who voted Green should both feel equally comfortable in this conversation, because the questions are about how they think, not what they think.

3. **Social desirability inoculation** — Q3 ("walk me through it honestly") and Q2 ("has there been a time you were wrong") are deliberately hard questions that push past socially desirable answers. Media literacy is a performance domain — people know what they're supposed to say about checking sources and being open-minded. The template is designed to get past the performance and into the reality. The interviewer guidance repeatedly instructs Alvia to probe when answers sound aspirational rather than actual. This is essential because a tool designed for how people say they consume information will fail; it has to be designed for how they actually do.

4. **Quantitative calibration point** — the scale question (Q4) creates a segmentation variable that can be cross-referenced against qualitative themes across all twelve interviews. The most analytically productive comparison will be between respondents' confidence ratings and their demonstrated behaviour in Q2-Q3. If high-confidence respondents show the same blind spots as low-confidence respondents, that challenges the assumption that the tool's primary audience is people who know they need help.

5. **Cross-interview context is the multiplier** — with it enabled, the AI interviewer can recognise when a respondent's experience fits or departs from emerging patterns. "Several people have described a similar moment — what's different about yours?" or "You're the first person who described actually looking up the primary source — what made you do that?" These comparisons make each successive interview more precise and more revealing. For research about information patterns across a population, this is not a nice-to-have; it's the mechanism that turns twelve individual conversations into a coherent picture of how UK citizens navigate political information.

6. **Direct design utility** — the template is structured so that Open Britain can read the output and answer three specific questions: (a) what are the common patterns in how people's political views are shaped without their awareness? (b) what do people actually do (versus what they say they do) when they encounter uncertain political claims? and (c) what would a non-partisan information tool need to look and feel like to be used by real people with real habits? Every question in the template feeds at least one of these three outputs. There is no filler.
