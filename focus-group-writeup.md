# Focus Group — AI Strategy Discovery: Project Write-Up

## Background

Focus Group is a £300m-revenue, 1,300-person UK technology services company headquartered in Shoreham-by-Sea, West Sussex. Founded in 2003 by Ralph Gilbert and Chris Goodman, it provides telecoms, IT managed services, cybersecurity, connectivity, and mobile solutions to approximately 30,000 SME customers across the UK. In April 2024, Hg Capital invested at a $1bn valuation — making Focus Group the latest British private-company unicorn. A new CEO, Neil Hall, joined in January 2025, and the company has pursued an aggressive acquisition strategy (Matrix247, Chalkline) to expand its managed services footprint.

Focus Group's six core service areas are: AI & Customer Experience, Telecoms (VoIP, Microsoft Teams phone), IT Solutions (cloud, Microsoft 365, disaster recovery), Cyber Security (awareness training, zero trust, SOC), Connectivity (broadband, fibre, leased lines, SD-WAN), and Mobile. They have already launched **Focus CX**, an AI-powered customer experience platform with virtual voice and chat agents, conversational analytics, omnichannel automation, and agentic AI capabilities for workflow execution.

The Hg investment brings Focus Group into a portfolio of 55+ software and services businesses collectively worth over $185bn. Critically, Hg operates **Catalyst** — an AI incubator with 80+ AI engineers, product managers, and designers that embeds directly into portfolio companies to build and ship agentic AI products. Across the Hg portfolio, AI initiatives are already delivering $130M+ in EBITDA uplift, with some portfolio companies seeing 40%+ booking increases from AI-enabled products in their first year. Focus Group has access to this entire ecosystem.

## The Role: Director of AI

Focus Group is hiring a **Director of AI**, reporting to the **Chief Strategy & Transformation Officer**, to lead the development and delivery of the company's AI strategy. The role is hybrid (1-2 days per week at Shoreham HQ) with competitive base salary, equity participation, and bonus.

### Responsibilities

- Lead Focus Group's AI strategy and roadmap, aligned to growth objectives
- Partner with business and technology leaders to embed AI across service delivery, customer experience, and internal operations
- Build and lead a small, high-impact AI team
- Evaluate and implement AI-driven tools and partnerships with clear commercial and operational benefits
- Design and launch new customer-facing value propositions powered by AI
- Define success metrics, governance, and value tracking for all AI initiatives
- Report progress and outcomes to the CSTO and executive leadership team

### What They Actually Want

The job specification is deliberately non-technical. There is no mention of ML engineering, model training, or data science pipelines. The essential requirements centre on: strategic and commercial problem-solving, consulting discipline combined with operational delivery, cross-functional change management, commercial acumen around AI's business value, high emotional intelligence, analytical skills, and genuine working knowledge of AI. Desirable experience includes B2B technology businesses, PE-backed or scaling organisations, and data/analytics/automation programmes.

This is a **commercial transformation role with AI as the lever**, not a technical AI role with commercial awareness bolted on. The person they need can move between the boardroom (Hg, the board, the CSTO) and the frontline (operations teams, service delivery, customer-facing staff) — and can translate AI's potential into measurable business outcomes in a PE-backed environment where every initiative needs to demonstrate EBITDA impact.

## Strategic Analysis

### What Focus Group Likely Needs From an AI Strategy

| Priority | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Internal operations efficiency** | 1,300 people serving 30,000 SMEs — cost-to-serve optimisation is the fast ROI play. Service delivery, support triage, onboarding, and account management are all automation candidates. |
| **Customer-facing AI products** | Focus CX is early. The revenue growth case is packaging AI capabilities that SMEs can't build themselves — AI as a service layer on top of existing telecoms/IT/connectivity. |
| **AI-augmented service delivery** | Differentiation from every other MSP and telecoms provider also claiming "AI-powered". The service itself needs to be demonstrably smarter, not just marketed differently. |
| **M&A integration acceleration** | Rapid acquisition pace (Matrix247, Chalkline, and likely more) means inconsistent systems, processes, and data. AI to standardise, integrate, and extract value faster. |
| **Hg Catalyst alignment** | Hg expects portfolio companies to productise AI for growth. Catalyst can embed AI engineering teams — but Focus Group needs the strategic direction and commercial framing to make that effective. |
| **Commercial proof points** | PE-backed = every initiative needs measurable EBITDA impact on a short timeline. The AI strategy must produce quantifiable wins, not innovation theatre. |

### Strategic Tensions the Leadership Team is Likely Navigating

These are the questions where leadership perspectives will diverge — and where divergence is itself the most valuable strategic data:

1. **Internal efficiency vs. customer products** — Where should the first investment go? Cost savings are faster to measure; new revenue is harder but more valuable to a growth narrative.

2. **Build vs. buy vs. partner** — Focus CX exists but is early. Does Focus Group build AI capability in-house, partner with vendors, leverage Hg Catalyst, or some combination? Each has different cost, speed, and control implications.

3. **Hg's ambition vs. organisational readiness** — Hg's portfolio is seeing transformational AI results. Does Focus Group have the data infrastructure, talent, and cultural readiness to match that pace? The gap between board-level ambition and operational reality is where strategies fail.

4. **Differentiation in a crowded market** — Every MSP and telecoms provider is putting "AI" on their website. What does Focus Group do that is genuinely distinctive and defensible?

5. **Cultural adoption at scale** — 1,300 people across multiple acquisitions, with different systems, processes, and levels of AI literacy. Strategy is easy; adoption at scale is hard.

6. **Speed vs. governance** — PE timelines demand speed. Responsible AI, data governance, and risk management demand rigour. The Director of AI has to deliver both simultaneously.

## Why This Research Exists

The covering email to Focus Group proposes something specific: instead of a standard first conversation, the candidate will set up a short Alvia session for the leadership team. Ten minutes per person, interviewed by an AI about what matters most to them on AI right now. The candidate comes away with real, structured insight — not assumptions — and uses it to develop a genuine first take on where AI should go for the business.

This is a deliberate strategic move. Every other candidate for this role will submit a CV and cover letter, possibly have a screening call, and eventually present a generic AI strategy framework in an interview. This approach is different in three ways:

1. **It demonstrates capability through action.** Alvia is a working AI product the candidate built. Proposing to use it on Focus Group's own leadership team shows the candidate can imagine, build, and deploy AI — not just talk about it.

2. **It produces genuinely useful output.** The interviews generate structured insight about what Focus Group's leaders actually care about, where they agree, where they diverge, and what they expect from the role. That's useful to Focus Group regardless of whether they hire the candidate.

3. **It reframes the power dynamic.** Instead of the candidate guessing what Focus Group needs and hoping the guess is right, the candidate gathers the data first and builds the proposal on evidence. This is exactly the approach the Director of AI role requires — commercial and analytical, not speculative.

## Project Configuration

**Project name:** Focus Group AI Strategy Discovery

**Project objective:** Understand how Focus Group's leadership team perceives the AI opportunity — where they see the highest-impact applications, what's blocking progress, how AI could strengthen the SME customer proposition, and what "success" looks like in the first year. Surface the real priorities, constraints, and expectations that should shape an actionable AI strategy.

**Audience context:** Focus Group senior leadership team — C-suite and functional heads across strategy, technology, commercial, operations, and customer experience. Likely a mix of AI enthusiasm levels: some will be deeply engaged, others pragmatically sceptical. All will be commercially minded and time-poor.

**Strategic context:** Focus Group is a £300m PE-backed technology services business at a growth inflection point. Backed by Hg Capital at a $1bn valuation, they serve 30,000 UK SMEs across telecoms, IT, cybersecurity, and connectivity. They've launched Focus CX (AI-powered customer experience platform) and have access to Hg's Catalyst AI incubator. They're hiring a Director of AI to own the strategy and roadmap. This research directly informs the development of a practical AI strategy proposal for the business — grounded in what their leadership team actually cares about, not generic frameworks. The interviewer should be curious and commercially sharp, treating each respondent as someone whose perspective genuinely matters to getting the strategy right.

**Context type:** Other — this is strategic advisory research for a specific business, not a product, content, marketing, or CX study in the conventional sense.

**Tone:** Conversational — these are senior leaders who are time-poor and accustomed to direct, substantive conversation. The tone needs to be warm but commercially credible; not deferential, not casual. Think "respected peer asking genuinely good questions" rather than "researcher conducting an interview."

**Cross-interview context:** Enabled. This is essential. The most valuable output from this research is not what any single leader thinks about AI — it's where the leadership team's views converge and diverge. When the CTO says "our data infrastructure is ready" and the COO says "we can't even get consistent reporting across acquired businesses," that gap is the single most important finding for strategy design. Cross-interview context lets Alvia recognise and probe these divergences in real time.

**PII summary redaction:** Enabled. Leaders may reference specific internal initiatives, personnel decisions, competitive intelligence, or board-level discussions. Redaction in summaries ensures the research output can be shared appropriately.

**Audio recording consent:** Enabled.

## Template Design

**Template name:** AI Strategy Leadership Interview

**Template objective:** Surface each leader's honest view on where AI should go at Focus Group — the opportunities they see, the blockers they experience, what their customers need, how ready the organisation actually is, and what they'd prioritise first. Build a complete picture of strategic alignment and divergence across the leadership team to inform a credible, grounded AI strategy proposal.

The template contains five questions (four open and one scale) designed to move from strategic vision (where's the opportunity?) through constraints (what's blocking progress?) and customer opportunity (what do SMEs need?) to readiness assessment (how prepared are we?) and finally priority definition (what should happen first?). The arc moves from aspiration through reality to action — each question grounds the next.

---

### Question 1: Strategic Opportunity

**Question:** "When you think about AI's potential for Focus Group specifically, what's the opportunity that excites you most — whether that's for your customers, your operations, or how you compete?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 90 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** Let them choose their own angle — don't steer toward internal vs. customer-facing. The natural direction they go reveals what they actually care about. If they give a generic answer ("AI will transform everything"), push for specificity: "Give me a concrete example — what would that actually look like at Focus Group?" Listen for whether they think about AI as cost reduction, revenue growth, competitive differentiation, or customer value. If they mention Focus CX or existing initiatives, explore what's working and what isn't. The gap between aspiration and current reality is valuable.

**Rationale:** This opening question maps each leader's mental model of AI's role in the business. The open framing — customers, operations, or competition — is deliberate; it doesn't lead the witness. Where each person naturally gravitates reveals their functional perspective and their implicit theory of AI value. A CTO who immediately talks about internal automation sees a different business from a CCO who talks about customer-facing AI products. Neither is wrong; the pattern across the leadership team reveals whether there's strategic alignment or a set of competing visions that the AI strategy will need to reconcile. For strategy design, knowing that the CSTO sees AI as a competitive differentiator while the COO sees it as an operations efficiency play is more useful than either view in isolation.

---

### Question 2: Real Blockers

**Question:** "What's actually getting in the way of that happening faster? What are the real blockers — whether that's people, data, technology, or just competing priorities?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 80 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This is the most important question for building a credible strategy. The first answer will usually be polished ("we need the right talent"). Push deeper: "Beyond that — what's the thing nobody talks about openly?" Listen for: data quality and fragmentation across acquired businesses, cultural resistance, lack of clear ownership, technical debt, unclear ROI frameworks, or tension between Hg's ambitions and operational reality. If they mention acquisitions (Matrix247, Chalkline), probe how integration complexity affects AI readiness. Don't let them stay abstract — get specific examples.

**Rationale:** Every AI strategy that fails does so not because the vision was wrong but because the constraints weren't honestly assessed. This question is the foundation of a credible proposal. A generic AI strategy says "implement an AI centre of excellence"; a grounded one says "before anything else, we need to solve the data fragmentation across three recently acquired businesses, because no AI initiative will deliver value on inconsistent data." The interviewer guidance to push past the polished first answer is critical — senior leaders are practised at giving boardroom-ready responses. The second or third thing they say, when they're past the performance, is where the real blockers live. Two follow-ups are allocated here because the layers take time to surface, and each layer reveals a different class of constraint (technical, cultural, political, resource-based).

---

### Question 3: Customer Opportunity

**Question:** "Your SME customers are probably hearing a lot about AI right now. What would it actually look like for Focus Group to be the company that makes AI useful for them?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 80 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This gets at the customer-facing product strategy and revenue growth case. SMEs typically can't afford enterprise AI — so what does Focus Group's unique position enable? Listen for whether they think about AI as something embedded invisibly in existing services (smarter support, predictive maintenance) or as distinct new products to sell. Probe on willingness to pay: "Do you think your customers would pay more for AI-powered services, or is it about reducing churn?" If they mention Focus CX, explore how customers are actually responding to it vs. the internal narrative. The commercial lens matters here.

**Rationale:** In a PE-backed business, the AI strategy ultimately needs to drive revenue growth — not just cost reduction. Focus Group's unique position is that it sits between AI capability and 30,000 SMEs who need AI but can't build it themselves. This question surfaces what each leader thinks that bridge looks like: is it embedded intelligence that makes existing services better (and therefore stickier), or is it distinct AI products that create new revenue lines? The distinction matters enormously for strategy, investment, and team structure. The probe on willingness to pay is essential because many technology companies build AI features that customers value but won't pay for — which creates cost without revenue. For the AI strategy proposal, the output from this question directly informs the commercial model: pricing, packaging, positioning, and the customer segments most likely to adopt.

---

### Question 4: Organisational Readiness

**Question:** "On a scale of 1 to 5, how ready do you think Focus Group is right now — across people, data, and technology — to execute on a serious AI strategy?"

**Type:** Scale (1-5) | **Time hint:** 50 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** The number is the starting point; the reasoning is the insight. High (4-5): probe what gives them that confidence — and where the weak spots are. "What's the one area that would drop that score?" Low (1-2): ask what specifically needs to change. Is it foundational (data infrastructure, talent) or cultural (adoption, buy-in)? Mid-range (3): often the most revealing — ask what would move it to a 4. If cross-interview context shows divergence (e.g., CTO says 4 but COO says 2), that gap IS the strategic insight. Connect their rating back to the blockers they mentioned earlier.

**Rationale:** The scale creates a quantitative anchor that can be compared across the leadership team. If six leaders give readiness scores ranging from 2 to 5, the distribution tells a story: either the organisation's readiness is genuinely uneven (strong technology, weak data, no AI talent) or leaders have fundamentally different perceptions of the same organisation. Both findings are strategically valuable. A readiness score of 2 from the CTO and 4 from the CSTO suggests a dangerous disconnect between strategic ambition and technical reality. A consistent 3 across the team suggests shared awareness of partial readiness — and the follow-up ("what would move it to a 4?") identifies the specific investments the strategy should prioritise. For the AI strategy proposal, this question produces a readiness heat map that grounds the phasing and sequencing of initiatives.

---

### Question 5: First Six Months

**Question:** "If you could fast-forward six months and the new AI strategy had delivered one thing that made you think 'this was worth it' — what would that one thing be?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 80 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This is the question that directly shapes the strategy proposal. It reveals what "success" actually looks like to each leader — and where expectations align or diverge. Push past vague answers ("delivered real value"): "Paint me the picture — what specifically has changed? What can you see or measure?" Listen for whether they want quick wins (cost savings, automation) or transformational outcomes (new revenue streams, market repositioning). If their answer contradicts the blockers from Q2 ("I want X but we can't do Y"), name that tension gently — it's a gift for the strategy. If cross-interview context shows the team wants different things, that alignment gap is the first strategic problem to solve.

**Rationale:** This is the highest-leverage question in the template because it directly defines the success criteria for the AI strategy's first phase. Every leader will have a different implicit definition of "worth it" — for the CFO it might be measurable cost reduction, for the CCO it might be a customer-facing AI product that drives upsell, for the COO it might be an automation that eliminates a painful manual process. The divergence between these answers is not a problem to smooth over; it is the core strategic challenge. An AI strategy that tries to satisfy six different definitions of success in six months will fail. A strategy that honestly names the divergence, makes a clear recommendation about sequencing, and explains why — that's what a Director of AI should deliver. The instruction to name contradictions with Q2 ("you said data fragmentation is the main blocker, but you want a customer-facing AI product in six months — how do you see that working?") is essential because it surfaces the gap between aspiration and constraint that the strategy must resolve.

Each 10-minute interview surfaces individual perspectives on AI priorities, blockers, customer opportunity, organisational readiness, and what success looks like — building a composite picture of strategic alignment and divergence to inform a credible AI strategy proposal.

**Target responses:** 6 — Focus Group's leadership team in a PE-backed, 1,300-person company likely comprises 6-8 senior leaders (CEO, CSTO, CTO, CCO, COO, CFO, plus possibly a Chief People Officer and heads of key business units). Six responses captures the core C-suite while keeping the ask manageable. The covering email proposes "your team gets interviewed" — six sessions at ten minutes each is a one-hour total commitment, which is reasonable for a hiring process where the candidate is also delivering genuine value.

**Voice provider:** OpenAI

**Additional questions:** 1 — keeps sessions tight at approximately 10 minutes as promised in the covering email, while allowing Barbara to pursue one thread that emerged naturally. In a leadership interview, the most productive additional question is likely to probe a specific tension or contradiction that surfaced during the five core questions — for example, if a leader expressed high ambition (Q1) but identified significant blockers (Q2) and gave a low readiness score (Q4), Barbara can ask directly how they reconcile those views.

**End-of-interview summary:** Enabled — generates a structured summary of themes, engagement quality, and objective satisfaction after each session. Essential for rapid synthesis across six leadership interviews. The summaries provide a consistent analytical layer that allows comparison of priorities and perspectives without requiring full transcript review.

**VAD eagerness:** Auto — these are senior leaders in substantive strategic conversation. They will pause to think, particularly on the blockers question (Q2) and the readiness assessment (Q4). Premature AI responses would break the reflective quality of the conversation and risk the interviewer appearing unsophisticated to an executive audience. Standard sensitivity gives the appropriate space.

## What This Research Produces

The five questions and cross-interview context are designed to produce specific, actionable outputs for the AI strategy proposal:

1. **A priority alignment matrix** (Q1 + Q5) — Where the leadership team agrees on AI's biggest opportunity and where they diverge. Agreement = mandate. Divergence = the first problem to solve.

2. **A genuine blockers inventory** (Q2) — Not the polished version from a board pack, but the real constraints as experienced by the people who would have to execute the strategy. Categorised by type: technical, cultural, resource, political, data.

3. **A customer opportunity thesis** (Q3) — What Focus Group's leaders believe their SME customers need from AI, whether customers would pay for it, and whether the opportunity is embedded intelligence (stickiness) or distinct products (new revenue).

4. **A readiness heat map** (Q4) — Comparable scores across the leadership team, cross-referenced with qualitative reasoning. Divergence between leaders is as valuable as the absolute scores.

5. **A success criteria framework** (Q5) — What each leader would consider "worth it" in six months, revealing whether the team has a shared definition of success or a set of competing expectations that the strategy must explicitly reconcile.

6. **An alignment diagnostic** (cross-interview synthesis) — The most valuable output of all. Where two or more leaders give contradictory views on the same question — one says "we're ready" while another says "we're nowhere near ready" — that gap is the single most important finding for the AI strategy, because no strategy survives a leadership team that disagrees on the starting conditions.

Together, these outputs allow the candidate to write an AI strategy proposal that no other applicant could produce — because it's built on what Focus Group's own leadership team said, not on generic frameworks applied from outside.
