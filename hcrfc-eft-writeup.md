# HCRFC Crowdfunding Supporter Research — Project Write-Up

## Background

Helensburgh Cricket and Rugby Football Club (HCRFC) is a community sports club in Helensburgh, Scotland, whose 1970s clubhouse was in need of significant energy upgrades. In 2025, the club was selected as one of only six organisations across the UK — and the only one in Scotland — to receive matchfunding through British Gas's Energy for Tomorrow programme, a Centrica-backed initiative that has committed £1m to helping community sports clubs reduce emissions and running costs.

The Energy for Tomorrow model is distinctive: for every £1 raised through crowdfunding, the programme contributed £3, with individual pledges matched up to £250. HCRFC secured a £34,000 matched grant from Scottish Gas, set an initial crowdfunding target of £51,000, and ultimately raised £60,000 — significantly outperforming the other four clubs in the 2025 cohort. Across all five clubs, the programme raised over £140,000 from more than 850 supporters.

The funds are being used for loft insulation, an energy-efficient boiler, LED pitch floodlights, and solar panels — upgrades projected to save the club £10,000 per year in energy costs.

## Why This Research Exists

HCRFC's campaign was a standout success. But from the programme's perspective, the question isn't just "what happened" — it's "why did it work so well, and can it be replicated?" Understanding what drove supporters to donate, how they experienced the campaign, and what they expect now it's over gives Energy for Tomorrow actionable insight for structuring future rounds. It also gives HCRFC itself a clearer picture of its supporter base and how to sustain engagement beyond the campaign.

## Project Configuration

**Project name:** What Makes People Back Community Energy Projects

**Project objective:** Understand what drives supporters to fund community energy and sustainability upgrades through crowdfunding; how much is about the club, how much is about the cause, and what role matchfunding plays in tipping people from interest to action.

**Audience context:** People who donated to the HCRFC Energy for Tomorrow crowdfunding campaign — a mix of club members, local residents, parents of junior players, businesses who offered rewards, and broader community supporters in and around Helensburgh.

**Strategic context:** HCRFC raised £60,000 against a £51,000 target, significantly outperforming other clubs in the Energy for Tomorrow programme. This research aims to understand why; what made this campaign resonate so strongly with supporters, and what can be learned for future rounds of the programme. Centrica has committed £1m to community sports energy projects, so understanding the supporter psychology behind successful campaigns directly informs how to structure, message, and support future projects across the UK.

**Tone:** Conversational — supporters will likely be enthusiastic about their club and the campaign. A warm, informal tone matches the community context.

**Cross-interview context:** Enabled. Patterns across different supporter types (long-standing members, parents, local businesses, community residents who aren't members) will be among the most valuable outputs of this research.

**PII redaction:** Enabled. Supporters may name other individuals, local businesses, or share personal financial details when discussing their donations.

## Template Design

**Template name:** Crowdfunding Supporter Deep Dive

**Template objective:** Understand what drives supporters to fund community energy and sustainability upgrades through crowdfunding; how much is about the club, how much is about the cause, and what role matchfunding plays in tipping people from interest to action. Uncover the real motivations behind donations; whether people were backing the club, the energy cause, or responding to the matchfunding mechanic, and what the experience of supporting felt like from their side.

The template contains five questions, designed to move from the concrete and personal (the moment of donation) through motivation and mechanics (why they gave and how matchfunding affected them) to community identity and forward-looking expectations.

---

### Question 1: The Trigger Moment

**Question:** "Tell me about the moment you decided to back the HCRFC crowdfunding campaign. What was going through your mind?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 70 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** Get them to a specific moment; not "I saw it and thought it was good" but WHERE they saw it, WHO told them, what made them stop and actually open their wallet. Was it a social media post, a conversation at the club, an email? The trigger matters. Push for whether they decided immediately or sat on it. If they're a club member, explore whether they felt obligated or genuinely excited. If they're not a member, that's even more interesting; what pulled an outsider in?

**Rationale:** This question identifies the channels and social dynamics that actually drove donations. Campaign organisers often assume they know what worked (the Facebook post, the email blast), but supporters' real triggers are frequently more personal — a conversation, a sense of obligation, seeing someone else donate. Understanding the actual decision moment, rather than a retrospective rationalisation, reveals what future campaigns should invest in.

---

### Question 2: Club vs Cause

**Question:** "When you think about why you donated, how much was it about the club itself versus the energy and sustainability side of things?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 60 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This is the key segmentation question. Some people backed the club they love and the energy angle was a bonus. Others care about sustainability and the club was the vehicle. And some were motivated primarily by the matchfunding multiplier. Don't let them say "a bit of both" without unpacking it. Ask: "If the campaign had been for new changing rooms instead of energy upgrades, would you still have donated the same amount?" That hypothetical reveals a lot. Listen for whether they even knew what the specific upgrades were (insulation, solar, LED floodlights) or just had a general sense.

**Rationale:** This is the strategic question for the entire Energy for Tomorrow programme. If supporters are primarily backing their club and the energy angle is incidental, then the programme's messaging should lead with community and belonging, not sustainability. If the energy cause genuinely motivates people independently, that changes the framing for clubs that don't have HCRFC's strong community identity. The hypothetical ("what if it were changing rooms?") forces supporters past the socially desirable answer and into what actually moved them.

---

### Question 3: The Matchfunding Effect

**Question:** "The campaign had matchfunding from British Gas, so your donation was effectively tripled. How much did that influence what you gave?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 60 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** Matchfunding is the programme's core mechanic, so understanding its psychological impact is critical. Did they donate more because of it? Would they have donated at all without it? Did it make them feel their contribution was more meaningful, or did they barely register it? Some people love the leverage ("my £20 becomes £60"), others are motivated by the urgency matchfunding creates ("we need to hit the target to unlock it"). Probe both angles. Also ask whether they understood how the matching worked; if they didn't, that's valuable feedback for future campaign messaging.

**Rationale:** Matchfunding is the central mechanism Centrica uses to amplify community giving, yet its psychological effect on individual donors is poorly understood. Does it increase donation size, donation likelihood, or both? Does the multiplier effect ("your money goes further") or the target urgency ("we must hit £51k to unlock the grant") do the heavier lifting? And critically — did supporters even understand how it worked? If the matching mechanic was confusing or invisible to many donors, that's a significant finding for how future campaigns are communicated.

---

### Question 4: Community Connection

**Question:** "On a scale of 1 to 5, how connected do you feel to what happens at the clubhouse, even outside of match days?"

**Type:** Scale (1-5) | **Time hint:** 50 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 1 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This measures community attachment beyond sport. A high score (4-5) suggests the club is a genuine community hub; ask what else they use it for or what it means to the area. A low score (1-2) from someone who still donated is fascinating; they backed a project for a place they rarely visit, so what drove that? Mid-range (3) often means "my kids go there" or "I used to be more involved"; explore whether the energy upgrades might change how they use the space. Connect their answer back to their donation motivation from Q1.

**Rationale:** The scale question serves two purposes. First, it creates a quantitative segmentation point — supporters can be grouped by connection level, and their qualitative answers from other questions can be analysed through that lens. Second, the conversation it opens is often more revealing than the number itself. A supporter who rates themselves a 2 but still donated £50 has a very different story from a 5 who gave the same amount. The question also tests whether the energy upgrades might expand the club's role as a community space beyond its current sporting function.

---

### Question 5: Expectations and Impact

**Question:** "Now the campaign's done, what would make you feel like your money was really well spent? What do you want to see happen?"

**Type:** Open | **Time hint:** 60 seconds | **Follow-ups:** 2 | **Required:** Yes

**Guidance to interviewer:** This surfaces expectations and what "success" looks like from the supporter's perspective. Some will want tangible proof (lower energy bills, solar panels visible on the roof). Others want to feel the community benefit (warmer clubhouse, more events, junior sections thriving). Some may not have thought about it at all; that's interesting too. If they mention wanting updates or communication, probe what format; would they read an email, watch a video, want to see the energy savings data? This directly informs post-campaign engagement strategy for future Energy for Tomorrow projects.

**Rationale:** Crowdfunding campaigns typically end with a "thank you" and then silence. This question captures what supporters actually want after they've given — which directly informs post-campaign engagement strategy. If supporters want to see energy savings data, that's a case for smart meters and dashboards. If they want to see community events, that's a case for programming support. If they haven't thought about it, that suggests the donation was primarily an emotional or social act rather than an investment in outcomes. Each answer type has different implications for how Energy for Tomorrow structures its post-funding support.

## Collection Configuration

**Collection name:** HCRFC Supporter Conversations — Pilot

**Description:** Pilot conversations with HCRFC crowdfunding supporters to understand donation motivations, the role of matchfunding, community connection, and expectations for impact; insights to inform future Energy for Tomorrow programme design.

**Target responses:** 15 — slightly higher than a standard pilot to capture the range of supporter types present in the HCRFC campaign (club members, parents of junior players, local businesses who offered rewards, and community residents who aren't club members).

**Voice provider:** OpenAI

**Additional questions:** Up to 2 — allows the AI interviewer to pursue threads that emerge naturally, such as whether supporters chose specific rewards, whether they shared the campaign with others, or whether they'd back another club's campaign in future.

**End-of-interview summary:** Enabled — generates a structured summary of themes, engagement quality, and objective satisfaction after each conversation.

**VAD eagerness:** Auto — supporters discussing their local club and community will likely be talkative and enthusiastic. The standard sensitivity avoids cutting people off mid-thought on topics they care about.

## Overall Design Rationale

The five questions follow a deliberate arc:

1. **Concrete to abstract** — starting with a specific moment (Q1) grounds the conversation in reality before moving to motivations (Q2-Q3) and broader identity (Q4-Q5).

2. **Three-way motivation decomposition** — Q1 through Q3 separate three overlapping drivers that the programme needs to understand independently: community loyalty, sustainability values, and matchfunding mechanics. Conflating them leads to campaigns that try to say everything and resonate with nothing.

3. **Quantitative anchor** — the scale question (Q4) provides a segmentation variable that can be cross-referenced against qualitative themes across all 15 interviews. "What do high-connection supporters care about that low-connection supporters don't?" is a question that only emerges from structured comparison.

4. **Forward-looking close** — Q5 shifts from reflection to expectation, which serves both the club (what should we communicate?) and the programme (what post-funding support should we offer?).

5. **Cross-interview context** is enabled because the most valuable insights will come from comparing patterns across supporter types, not from any single conversation. When the AI interviewer can reference themes emerging from earlier interviews, it can probe whether a given supporter's experience is typical or unusual.

The template is designed to produce insights that are actionable at two levels: for HCRFC (understanding their supporter base and sustaining engagement) and for Energy for Tomorrow (understanding what made this campaign outperform and how to replicate it across future cohorts of community sports clubs).
